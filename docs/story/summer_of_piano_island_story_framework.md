# Summer of Piano Island — Story Framework

Read [`../design_brief.md`](../design_brief.md) first for the minimum-token summary. This file is the single source of truth for Kulangsu's story direction and narrative framing.

Keep implementation state machines, quest plumbing, UI flow, and save behavior in the other docs that own those systems.

## Canon Position

Kulangsu's current playable canon remains the five-landmark melody-driven island game:

- ferry arrival framing
- Piano Ferry, Trinity Church, Bi Shan Tunnel, Long Shan Tunnel, and Bagua Tower
- four melody fragments
- final harbor performance
- departure-or-stay ending choice

This story framework does not replace that route. It explains what that route means.

The current playable game should be read as a music-centered slice of a broader coming-of-age story about memory, guilt, family sacrifice, identity, preservation, and belonging.

## Title

**Summer of Piano Island**

## Core Premise

A Year 12 boy returns to Kulangsu during the emotionally charged stretch leading into his final school year and the national university entrance exam.

He has grown up on the island, but he no longer hears it clearly. Family distance, grief after his grandmother's death, exam pressure, and uncertainty about the future have left him inwardly estranged from home.

When he arrives by ferry, the island feels strangely quiet. The old harbor festival tune no longer fully carries through its landmarks, residents, and routines. Parts of the island's shared musical memory have faded into fragments.

Over the course of the game, restoring the melody becomes both:

- an outward act of helping the island reconnect to itself
- an inward act of relearning memory, inheritance, and home

The story is about memory, guilt, family sacrifice, identity, pressure, preservation, and quiet belonging.

## Main Character

A Year 12 boy living on Piano Island.

He is intelligent, sensitive, and under heavy expectation.

He loves:

- music
- sports
- comic books
- 4WD model cars
- robot models

He admires martial arts masters because they seem to possess the discipline, calm, and certainty he lacks.

He is interested in many possible futures:

- architecture
- computer science
- politics / foreign affairs
- sports
- music

He does not truly know what he wants.

He also has a chance to be pre-selected into a well-known university for a politics-related major connected to foreign affairs, which others see as prestigious, but he is unsure whether that path belongs to him.

### Core Inner Struggle

He is preparing for an exam that may decide his future, while not knowing which future is truly his.

## Family Background

The family returned to the island when he was in **Year 5**, after job changes forced his parents to leave better positions in the provincial capital and return to his father's hometown.

At about the same time, his grandmother began suffering from Alzheimer's and could no longer care for herself. Because both parents were often away for work, they hired **A Po**, a woman in her 60s, to care for both the grandmother and the boy.

After the grandmother passed away peacefully at the end of Year 11, A Po stayed on and continued looking after the boy.

### Father

- works in another city
- only comes home during Lunar New Year / Spring Festival
- respected, steady, responsible
- once lost the career he loved because of bureaucracy
- spent a year working in another industry to support the family
- during the boy's final school year, finally gets a chance to return to the work he loves

### Mother

- works with a sports team
- usually only comes home from Saturday afternoon to Sunday afternoon
- hardworking, disciplined, respected
- during this same year, is also pushing toward a major opportunity in her career

### A Po

- practical, loyal, warm in action more than words
- cared for both grandmother and grandson for years
- becomes the emotional anchor of the house

## Story Logic For The Current Game

The five-landmark game should be read with this protagonist framing:

- he belongs to the island, but has become emotionally distant from it
- the ferry arrival marks re-entry rather than first discovery
- he knows the island in fragments, the way someone knows home after growing away from it
- restoring the melody is also a way of restoring his ability to hear home truthfully

The melody is not just a quest object. It is the public form of what the story is about: memory that survives only when someone listens, carries it, and gives it back to others.

## The Four Continuous Story Lines

The current game should continuously advance these four lines through landmark play, resident dialogue, journal writing, and ending tone.

### 1. Grandmother: Memory, Guilt, and Grace

This is the emotional heart of the story.

The grandmother was once:

- educated
- kind
- deeply faithful
- from a wealthy Southeast Asian family

She returned to the island with the man she loved, later her husband, a gifted painter. After he died young during the war for lack of medical resources, she raised three children alone without depending on her wealthy family, sustained by dignity and faith.

By the time the boy came back to the island in Year 5, Alzheimer's had already begun taking away her memory and daily functioning.

As a child, he did not understand her illness. He was naughty, selfish, and thought himself clever. He laughed at her confusion, took advantage of her forgetfulness, and sometimes stole money from her bag after church, believing she would never remember.

She used to take him to church every Sunday. He cared more about the pocket money after Sunday school than about faith, love, or her effort.

After her death, those memories return and become a source of deep guilt. What once seemed small now feels unforgivable.

In the current game, this line should be carried primarily through:

- church routine
- remembered songs
- harbor and ferry sounds
- ordinary objects and repeated paths
- the realization that the island's lost music is bound up with love that was not fully understood in time

### Emotional Movement

forgotten cruelty -> memory returning -> shame -> grief -> longing -> inner change

### 2. Parents: Absence, Sacrifice, and Re-understanding

At first, the boy experiences his parents mainly as absence.

His father is almost never there.
His mother is home only briefly on weekends.
As a child, this feels like abandonment.

But over the course of the year, especially under the pressure of the entrance exam and graduation, he slowly begins to understand the truth:

- they were never careless
- they never stopped loving him
- they endured difficult career losses and rigid systems
- they kept the family standing through sacrifice, distance, and restraint
- they protected him from much of their struggle

They are respected by others, never openly bitter, and never fight in front of him. The boy gradually sees that they were not choosing work over family in a selfish way; they were trying to hold together both livelihood and dignity at the same time.

In the current game, this line should be carried primarily through:

- ferry arrival and departure framing
- the island's recurring imagery of leaving and returning
- resident subtext around work, steadiness, and care
- ending tone rather than direct confrontation-heavy scenes

### Emotional Movement

resentment -> questioning -> understanding -> respect -> partial emotional reconciliation

### 3. The Boy: Exam Pressure, Identity, and the Fear of Choosing Wrong

This is the main coming-of-age line.

The national university entrance exam hangs over the entire year. Teachers, neighbors, family friends, and relatives all treat it as the defining event of his life. Every mock exam, ranking, and application conversation increases the pressure.

Because he is capable and talented, people project their hopes onto him:

- a prestigious university
- a politics-related path
- a stable and admired profession
- success that proves the family's sacrifices were worth it

But he still does not know who he is supposed to become.

Architecture attracts him because of beauty, design, and the island's old buildings.
Computer science suggests practicality and a modern future.
Politics / foreign affairs offers prestige and recognition.
Music and sports connect to joy, talent, and a different kind of self.

The exam becomes more than an academic event. It becomes a symbol of his fear:

**What if I choose the wrong life?**

His four close friends help define this struggle. They share hobbies and deep familiarity, but each is moving toward a different future. Their friendship gives him comfort, but also sharpens the feeling that everyone else may be finding direction faster than he is.

In the current game, this line should appear through:

- journal reflection
- optional resident beats
- Bagua's perspective and synthesis framing
- the emotional meaning of whether to leave on the morning ferry or stay a little longer

### Emotional Movement

performance -> confusion -> pressure -> self-questioning -> quiet honesty

### 4. Architecture: Preservation, Memory, and Inheritance

This storyline mirrors the grandmother's arc.

Just as the boy failed to fully value his grandmother before losing her, he begins to realize that the island's old buildings may also disappear through neglect, commercial pressure, or indifference before people truly understand what they hold.

He gradually learns to see the architecture not just as scenery, but as:

- memory made visible
- inherited beauty
- evidence of layered lives
- something fragile that must be noticed and protected

This line can also gently connect to his uncertainty about the future, especially architecture as one possible path, without forcing a final answer too early.

In the current game, this line should be anchored in:

- Trinity Church
- Bagua Tower
- the tunnels as layered passage and hidden history
- the broader built environment as memory made visible

### Emotional Movement

familiarity -> curiosity -> emotional connection -> responsibility

## Landmark Mapping For The Current Playable Route

Each landmark should carry both its gameplay role and one part of the broader framework.

### Piano Ferry

Ferry Plaza is both the opening frame and the ending frame.

The player arrives here, notices the unusual quiet, meets Caretaker Lian, and hears the harbor refrain more clearly near the old piano crate. This district establishes that music on Kulangsu is not just performance. It is memory, orientation, social connection, and the feeling of home as both return and departure.

It is also the best place to carry the family-distance line. The ferry image should quietly suggest work, leaving, coming back, and the emotional cost of not knowing how to speak honestly to the people you love.

### Trinity Church

Trinity Church is the first full lesson in listening.

The church arc is about harmony, memory, guilt, and grace. By following the scattered choir cues and returning to Mei, the player learns that the island's missing music can still be found in ordered traces. This is the landmark that should carry the grandmother line most clearly: church routine, remembered sound, and the painful realization that love was present even when it was not fully understood.

The reward is not only the first fragment, but the realization that the melody is distributed through people and places rather than lost outright.

### Bi Shan Tunnel

Bi Shan Tunnel turns the story inward.

This arc emphasizes uncertainty, echo, hidden passage, and the inward pressure of memory. The player follows reflected cues through a disorienting space and learns that memory on the island does not always present itself directly. Some parts of the melody must be recovered through attention, patience, and the willingness to enter what has been avoided.

This is the landmark that most naturally carries shame, suppression, and the fear of facing what has been buried.

### Long Shan Tunnel

Long Shan Tunnel shifts the story from listening to companionship.

Instead of simply tracing echoes, the player helps another person cross safely. This landmark makes trust and reassurance part of the restoration arc. The recovered fragment matters, but so does the fact that the player becomes someone residents can rely on.

This should quietly echo the story's family and friendship lines: care is often practical, unglamorous, and steadying rather than dramatic.

### Bagua Tower

Bagua Tower is the synthesis point.

After learning the island piece by piece, the player finally gains enough perspective to assemble the fragments into a coherent whole. The tower should feel like revelation rather than escalation for its own sake: the player can now read the island as one connected composition instead of a string of isolated tasks.

This landmark should carry the architecture and inheritance line most strongly. From here, the island's built spaces stop reading as backdrop and begin reading as something worth noticing, valuing, and carrying forward.

## Deferred Expansion Material

The next sections are future-facing background for later content growth. They are part of the broader story framework, but they are not required current-playable canon unless a later implementation task explicitly pulls them into the landmark game.

The active playable canon remains the five-landmark route summarized in `Current Playable Story Arc` below.

## Supporting Characters For Future Expansion

- **Mr. Lin** — retired art / architecture teacher who teaches the boy how to really see buildings
- **Pastor Chen / Sister Agnes** — linked to grandmother's church life and memory
- **Uncle Zhao** — elderly owner of a fading villa who resists selling it
- **Mei** — girl his age from a family skilled in traditional repair work
- **Mr. Huang** — businessman pushing modernization / commercial logic
- **Professor Xu** — historian who helps frame the island's layered past
- **Old Madam Wei** — ordinary resident whose house carries everyday memory

These characters should each do more than give information. They should embody different relationships to memory, preservation, change, labor, inheritance, and practical reality.

## The Four Friends

- **The practical one** — already knows what he wants
- **The dreamer** — creative, idealistic, uncertain in a different way
- **The rebel** — acts indifferent to the future but hides fear
- **The loyal one** — grounded, emotionally steady, values friendship most

Together they show that even among close friends, adulthood begins to pull people apart.

These friendships are part of the broader framework, but should remain background or light-touch material until the current island build is ready to support them directly.

## Current Playable Story Arc

The current canonical story arc is:

1. Return to the island by ferry and notice how quiet and emotionally distant it has become.
2. Discover that residents, places, and routines still carry partial traces of the old festival melody.
3. Help each landmark district resolve its local tension and recover a fragment of the tune.
4. Reconstruct the melody as both a public island memory and a private emotional reckoning.
5. Perform the restored tune at Ferry Plaza and let the island answer back.
6. Choose whether to leave on the morning ferry or stay a little longer, now with a clearer sense of truth, inheritance, and belonging.

The story should feel calm, intimate, and place-driven throughout. Even when the player is progressing the main quest, the emotional tone should stay closer to listening and belonging than to urgency or danger.

## Central Mystery

The island's quiet is the story's main mystery.

That quiet should be read in two linked ways:

- outwardly, the old harbor festival tune no longer fully lives in the island's shared routine
- inwardly, the protagonist has become unable to hear home clearly because of grief, guilt, pressure, and distance

The cause should remain gentle rather than over-explained. What matters is the felt truth:

- the island is missing part of its musical memory
- the boy's relationship to home is also fragmented
- residents remember pieces of the tune differently
- landmarks hold onto those fragments through sound, routine, and place
- restoring the melody helps both the island and the protagonist feel connected again

## Festival And Resolution

Once the melody is restored, the player returns to Ferry Plaza for the harbor-stage performance.

This sequence is the emotional payoff for the whole journey. The island responds through light, sound, gathered residents, and a renewed sense of presence. The festival should feel like the island remembering itself in public, and like the protagonist finally hearing home clearly again.

The ending then asks a simple personal question:

- leave on the morning ferry
- stay a little longer

He does not need a perfect answer about career or adulthood, but he should now be able to choose with more honesty than avoidance.

## Ending Tones

The story supports three ending tones:

- Quiet maturity: the melody is restored and the player departs with a clearer, more honest sense of self
- Relationship emphasis: stronger resident turnout and community payoff emphasize belonging and reconnection
- Inheritance emphasis: the player stays, and postgame exploration continues in a lighter resonant state tied to place, memory, and what is worth carrying forward

These are tonal variations on the same core story, not three separate branching plots.

## Part I-V Seasonal Background Frame

This broader year-long structure sits behind the current playable slice. It should inform future expansion, but it should not force a structural rewrite of the current landmark game.

### Part I — Summer: The Empty House

**Time:** First summer after Grandma's death, before Year 12 begins.

The boy enters the long final year of high school in a house that no longer feels whole. Grandma passed away peacefully at the end of Year 11. A Po is still there, cooking, cleaning, and keeping daily life running, but the house feels strangely hollow without the woman whose illness had shaped its rhythm for years.

The boy does not openly grieve. Instead, he stays restless and distracted, thinking about school, hobbies, friends, and the coming pressure of the national university entrance exam. His parents remain mostly absent: his father far away, his mother home only briefly on weekends. He tells himself he is used to it.

Beneath the surface, though, small memories begin to stir: church bells, old piano sounds, Grandma's chair, her bag, her voice. He avoids them.

**Function:** establish loss, emotional numbness, family distance, and the exam year ahead.

### Part II — Autumn: The Weight of Expectation

**Time:** Beginning of Year 12.

School becomes serious. Teachers, classmates, neighbors, and relatives all speak about the national entrance exam as though it will decide everything. Rankings, mock exams, and future plans begin closing in on him. Because he is capable, people expect a lot from him.

He is offered or considered for a promising path into a prestigious university through a politics-related major connected to foreign affairs. To others, it looks like a rare opportunity. To him, it feels like another life being suggested for him before he has even understood his own.

At the same time, his inner uncertainty grows. He likes music, sports, architecture, drawing, and other possible futures, but cannot tell which one is truly his. His four close friends become a key part of this section: they share comics, model cars, robot kits, jokes, and daily companionship, yet each begins to lean toward a different future.

The island's architecture line can also begin more clearly here. Through school, church, a neighbor, or an older mentor, he starts noticing the old buildings around him not just as background scenery, but as something with character and history.

**Function:** build pressure, future anxiety, and the first external pull toward identity questions.

### Part III — Winter: Memory Returns

**Time:** Mid-year, colder season, inward emotional turn.

Winter brings a quieter mood, and the boy has fewer ways to outrun himself. Memories of Grandma return more sharply. He begins to remember specific moments from childhood that he once treated lightly but now sees with shame: making fun of her confusion, using her illness for his own advantage, pretending kindness so he could steal money from her bag after church.

This is also where the story deepens Grandma's real history. Through A Po, church connections, family stories, photographs, old objects, or conversations, the boy begins to understand who she had been before Alzheimer's: educated, dignified, faithful, from a wealthy Southeast Asian family, widow of a gifted painter, mother who raised three children alone without begging help from others.

At the same time, he starts seeing more of his parents' truth as well. Their absence no longer looks so simple. Hints of his father's lost career, his mother's struggle, and the cost of holding the family together begin to come into view.

The architecture thread also grows in emotional meaning here: old buildings become connected not just to beauty, but to memory, endurance, and inherited lives.

**Function:** deepen guilt, enlarge the grandmother's humanity, and widen the boy's understanding of family sacrifice.

### Part IV — Spring Festival and Spring: The First New Year Without Grandma

**Time:** Spring Festival through the months leading closer to the exam.

This is the emotional center of the second half of the story.

The father returns home for Spring Festival, as he does only once a year. It is the **first Spring Festival without Grandma**, and her absence is felt everywhere. The holiday meal is different. Familiar preparations feel incomplete. A Po may try to continue old routines, but that only makes the empty space clearer. A church visit, a family custom, or even a simple object can trigger painful memories.

For the boy, reunion is not simple comfort. The family is briefly together, yet he feels how long they have lived in fragments. He sees his parents with both closeness and strangeness. He feels warmth, but also distance. It becomes harder to keep viewing them only as absent adults; now they are people shaped by burdens, disappointments, and hopes of their own.

Spring Festival is also the point where the boy's guilt toward Grandma becomes hardest to avoid. Holiday remembrance, church, or the father's presence may force him to confront the love he had received but failed to understand when he was younger.

After the festival, spring pushes everything forward. The exam draws nearer. Conversations about universities and future paths become unavoidable. Friends begin moving more clearly toward different directions. The boy becomes more involved in the architecture-preservation thread and starts to act rather than only observe. He may help document a building, speak to an elder, sketch architecture, or become attached to a threatened place.

He still does not fully know his future, but this is the section where he begins rejecting the idea of living only to satisfy expectations.

**Function:** make family reunion emotionally complex, bring the father fully into the story, intensify Grandma's absence, and turn reflection into change.

### Part V — Summer: After the Exam

**Time:** Exam season, graduation, arrival of the next summer.

The exam finally arrives and passes. The months of pressure break open into an unfamiliar quiet. Nothing is fully solved, but something important has changed: the boy is no longer only being carried forward by pressure, guilt, and other people's expectations.

By now, he sees Grandma differently, not only as a source of regret, but as someone whose strength, faith, and quiet endurance have become part of what formed him. He sees his parents differently too: still imperfectly close, still marked by years of absence, but no longer reduced in his mind to what they failed to give. He understands more of what they carried.

His friendships also begin to change. Graduation means the group will not remain exactly as it was. Their shared hobbies and boyhood closeness now stand beside the first real feeling of separation.

The island itself also feels transformed. The old buildings are no longer just familiar scenery. They have become a visible reminder that memory survives only if someone pays attention.

He does not need to end the story with one perfect answer about his career. It may be stronger if he ends with more honesty than certainty. He may still not know whether he will become an architect, enter another field, or keep searching. But he now knows he must choose in truth, not simply drift into what others want.

The next summer arrives, and the island no longer feels like the place where he was left behind. It feels like the place that raised him.

**Function:** release the exam pressure, complete emotional maturation, and leave the future open but hopeful.

## Simple Emotional Arc Across The Five Parts

- **Part I:** loss without full feeling
- **Part II:** pressure without clarity
- **Part III:** memory without escape
- **Part IV:** reunion without Grandma, choice without certainty
- **Part V:** future without full answers, but with growth

## Notes On Background History

The liberation / campaign history of Xiamen and related 1949 events should remain **background setting information only** for now, and stay **out of the active story plot** unless added later.

## Narrative Rules

- Keep the story calm, local, and place-driven.
- Keep the main conflict centered on memory, connection, guilt, family, identity, and restoration rather than villainy.
- Keep the ferry as the framing image for both arrival and departure.
- Keep the five-landmark route canonical unless the project makes an explicit world-design change.
- Let residents and landmarks carry the story more than exposition dumps or detached cutscenes.
- Treat the final performance as a communal ritual, not a victory screen.
- Treat the current playable route as a music-centered slice of the broader year-long framework, not as a separate unrelated premise.
